# Chemotherapy-induced ovarian suppression (CIOS)

## Explanation

The hypothesis that **a substantial fraction of the apparent "chemotherapy benefit" in premenopausal HR+/HER2− breast cancer is mediated by chemotherapy's effect on the ovaries** — inducing temporary or permanent amenorrhea and thereby suppressing estrogen-driven tumor growth — rather than by direct cytotoxic killing of cancer cells. If true, then **OFS + endocrine therapy (without chemotherapy)** should produce comparable outcomes to chemotherapy + endocrine therapy in this population, because OFS achieves estrogen suppression directly. The question is genuinely unresolved as of 2025–2026 and is the central clinical/scientific question motivating [[ofset-trial]].

## Key points

- **Origin of the hypothesis: [[rxponder-trial]] (2021)** — chemo-endocrine therapy outperformed endocrine therapy alone in premenopausal women but **not** in postmenopausal women. The cleanest mechanistic explanation: chemo's premenopausal-specific benefit is from inducing ovarian suppression, which postmenopausal women already have [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]].
- **Supporting evidence:**
  - Patients in RxPONDER who became amenorrheic had better outcomes regardless of arm [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]]
  - **June 2024 [[anti-mullerian-hormone]] post-hoc analysis (RxPONDER):** women with **low AMH** (less ovarian reserve to suppress) **derived less chemotherapy benefit** than those with medium/high AMH [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]] — exactly the pattern CIOS predicts
- **Counter-evidence:**
  - Initial RxPONDER analysis: **OFS rates were similar in both arms over time**, so CIOS could not be established as the *sole* mechanism on the strength of that data alone [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]]
  - Some chemo benefit may be direct cytotoxic effect, particularly in higher-grade tumors
- **The pivotal pending answer: [[ofset-trial]] (NCT05879926).** By giving OFS in **both** arms and adding chemotherapy only to one, OFSET isolates whether chemo adds anything beyond ovarian suppression. If chemo loses its benefit when OFS is universal, CIOS is the mechanism. If chemo retains benefit, direct cytotoxicity is real.
- **Practical implication today:** in premenopausal HR+/HER2− patients where the chemo decision is borderline (the Q4-style intermediate-RS scenarios), **OFS + AI is a defensible non-chemotherapy strategy** because it directly produces the suppression that chemo may have been working through. NCCN/ASCO/ESMO have not formally endorsed OFS+AI as equivalent to chemo+endocrine therapy in this population, but neither do they prohibit it.
- **Q4-relevant nuance:** the Q4 patient is **node-negative**; the CIOS hypothesis was generated from **node-positive** data ([[rxponder-trial]]). Whether CIOS extends cleanly to the [[tailorx-trial]] premenopausal high-risk-clinical-features subgroup is an additional layer of uncertainty. See [[node-negative-vs-node-positive-genomic-trial-scoping]].

## What the wiki cannot tell you

Whether CIOS or direct cytotoxicity dominates the chemo benefit in the Q4 population. The data don't say. **OFSET will say.** Until then, the choice between chemo+endocrine therapy and OFS+AI is a judgment call informed by:
- The patient's tolerance for chemotherapy toxicity
- The patient's tolerance for OFS-induced menopausal symptoms
- The patient's fertility goals
- The user's prior beliefs about CIOS as a mechanism
