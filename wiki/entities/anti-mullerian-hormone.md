# Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)

## Overview

A glycoprotein hormone produced by ovarian granulosa cells that serves as a **biomarker of ovarian reserve**. In premenopausal HR+/HER2− breast cancer, AMH has emerged as a candidate **predictive biomarker** for chemotherapy benefit — specifically, as evidence supporting the [[chemotherapy-induced-ovarian-suppression]] (CIOS) hypothesis.

## Key facts

- **Operational interpretation:**
  - **Higher AMH** → more ovarian reserve, more potential to be suppressed by chemotherapy
  - **Lower AMH** → less ovarian reserve, less to suppress
- **June 2024 [[rxponder-trial]] post-hoc analysis:** premenopausal women with **low baseline AMH derived less benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy** compared to those with medium or high AMH levels [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]]. This is the strongest pro-CIOS evidence to date — if chemo's benefit were purely cytotoxic, AMH wouldn't predict response.
- **Better than self-report:** the same analysis emphasized that AMH is a **stronger predictor of chemotherapy response than self-reported menopausal status or age** [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]].
- **Status:** retrospective post-hoc finding from RxPONDER. Has not been prospectively validated as a treatment-decision biomarker. NCCN/ASCO/ESMO do not currently incorporate AMH into adjuvant decision-making for HR+/HER2− breast cancer.
- **Q4 relevance:** AMH testing is *not* required to make the Q4 decision and is not standard. But if the user/patient is willing to consider OFS+AI in lieu of chemo, an AMH measurement might tip a borderline decision: a low-AMH 39yo (already approaching reduced ovarian reserve) might gain less from chemotherapy by the CIOS hypothesis, strengthening the case for OFS+AI.
