# Aromatase inhibitor (AI)

## Overview

Drug class that blocks aromatase — the enzyme converting androgens to estrogens in peripheral tissues. **Postmenopausal women** rely on aromatase activity for estrogen production (their ovaries no longer produce significant estradiol), which is why AIs work as monotherapy postmenopausally. **Premenopausal women** still produce estradiol from the ovaries directly, so AIs alone are *insufficient* — they require concurrent [[ovarian-function-suppression]] to be effective.

## Key facts

- **Three AIs in clinical use:**
  - **Anastrozole** (Arimidex) — non-steroidal
  - **Letrozole** (Femara) — non-steroidal
  - **Exemestane** (Aromasin) — steroidal; the agent used in [[soft-text-trials|SOFT/TEXT]]
- **Mechanism:** competitive (non-steroidal) or irreversible (exemestane) inhibition of aromatase, lowering peripheral and intratumoral estrogen.
- **Premenopausal use requires [[ovarian-function-suppression]].** AIs alone in premenopausal women cause a paradoxical FSH/LH surge that can transiently *increase* ovarian estrogen — clinically harmful. Pairing with OFS prevents this.
- **Pivotal premenopausal evidence ([[soft-text-trials|SOFT/TEXT]]):** OFS + exemestane reduced recurrence vs OFS + tamoxifen in high-risk premenopausal HR+ disease [[premenopausal-intermediate-oncotype-2026]]. The "OFS + AI" arm of the Q4 decision is anchored on this evidence.
- **Toxicity profile:** musculoskeletal (arthralgia, myalgia), bone-density loss (synergistic with OFS), vasomotor symptoms, vaginal atrophy. Bone-health monitoring + DXA + bisphosphonates often indicated.
- **Q4 relevance:** if OFS+AI is chosen over chemo+endocrine therapy, the AI in the regimen would typically be exemestane (per SOFT/TEXT) or letrozole (per general practice). The class effect is the principal driver, not the specific agent.
