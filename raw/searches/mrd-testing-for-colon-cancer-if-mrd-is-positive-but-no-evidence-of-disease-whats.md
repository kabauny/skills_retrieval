# MRD testing for colon  cancer. If MRD is positive, but no evidence of disease, what's the current evidence for starting chemotherapy vs just observe until measurable disease can be detected 

Based on current oncology guidelines and recent clinical trials (as of 2025–2026), the management of colon cancer patients who are Minimal Residual Disease (MRD) positive via circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) but have no radiographic evidence of disease (NED) remains a heavily researched, yet transitional, area of practice. 

Here is the current evidence regarding whether to start chemotherapy versus observe until measurable disease is detected:

### 1. Prognostic Value of a Positive MRD Test
A positive ctDNA/MRD test in the setting of NED is strongly prognostic. Patients with persistent MRD positivity post-surgery have a near 100% chance of eventual clinical recurrence if no systemic therapy is given [search-sourced]. Furthermore, molecular recurrence (ctDNA positivity) typically predates visible radiographic recurrence by a median lead time of 5 to 8 months [search-sourced]. 

### 2. Current Clinical Guidelines
Despite its high prognostic accuracy, **2025 updates to the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) Guidelines** maintain that while ctDNA is recognized as a high-risk prognostic marker for recurrence, there is currently **insufficient evidence to recommend its routine use for treatment decision-making outside of clinical trials** [search-sourced]. The NCCN explicitly states that care escalation (starting chemotherapy) or de-escalation based solely on ctDNA findings is not recommended as the standard of care [search-sourced].

### 3. Evidence in the Immediate Post-Operative (Adjuvant) Setting
If a patient is MRD-positive shortly after curative-intent surgery with clear scans, the evidence for chemotherapy is mixed depending on trial design:
*   **Support for Chemotherapy (GALAXY, BESPOKE, & DYNAMIC trials):** Large observational studies (GALAXY and BESPOKE) found that patients with detectable MRD who were treated with adjuvant chemotherapy had significantly better disease-free survival and ctDNA clearance rates compared to those who were only observed [search-sourced]. The landmark DYNAMIC trial in Stage II colon cancer also demonstrated that treating ctDNA-positive patients with adjuvant chemotherapy resulted in highly favorable 3-year recurrence-free survival, while sparing ctDNA-negative patients from toxicity [search-sourced].
*   **Arguments for Observation/Futility (COBRA Trial):** The prospective randomized Phase II/III COBRA trial (NRG-GI005) tested whether ctDNA-directed chemotherapy improved outcomes in low-risk stage II colon cancer patients. It was halted early for futility in 2024 because 6 months of adjuvant chemotherapy failed to improve ctDNA clearance compared to active surveillance alone (clearance was 11% in the chemotherapy arm vs. 43% in the observation arm) [search-sourced].

### 4. Evidence in the Longitudinal Surveillance Setting
If a patient has already completed initial treatments and their MRD status turns positive during routine follow-up while imaging remains negative, the data for initiating early chemotherapy is lacking:
*   **ALTAIR Trial:** This trial specifically investigated whether starting systemic therapy (trifluridine/tipiracil) versus a placebo in resected colorectal cancer patients who developed positive MRD during surveillance improved outcomes. The trial showed **no significant difference in overall disease-free survival** between the two groups [search-sourced]. This suggests that intervening with chemotherapy based solely on molecular recurrence may not provide a definitive advantage over waiting for measurable disease to appear.

### Summary Conclusion
While an MRD-positive result is a powerful harbinger of eventual relapse, prospectively validated randomized trials (like COBRA and ALTAIR) have largely failed to prove that initiating chemotherapy purely based on a positive blood test improves outcomes over standard observation [search-sourced]. Consequently, starting chemotherapy strictly based on a positive MRD test without radiologic evidence of disease is not currently recommended by standard guidelines; standard clinical and pathologic features should continue to dictate treatment until definitive clinical trial results mature [search-sourced].
